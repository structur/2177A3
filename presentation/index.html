Case Study

DRD is a large department in a public research university, its essential processes are teaching and research:

  • supporting ~30 labs performing research across its discipline
  • stewarding large volumes of information
  • using many information systems

Business Context

  • Over $2 billion in revenue and over $1 billion annually-awarded research funds
  • Over 80,000 students, 1,900 faculty and 14,000 staff [Facts & Figures, 2014].
Customers: Teaching: University, Faculty, Students Research: Researchers, Other Departments & Partners, Granting bodies Research Activities
  • Research (Publications Metric)
Competition:
  • Competition between institution against the backdrop of informal and formal collaborations
  • For funding is perhaps the strongest, limited and rivalrous nature of grants
Information Management:
  • Dense matrix of university-wide policies from many governing bodies, emphasis on research ethics
  • Provincial and Federal Regulations (PIPEDA, Digital Privacy Act [S-4], PHIPA)

Organization Context

objectives & strategies
  • Support the multiple labs that compose DRD with an eye on facilitating research publications and meaningful results
  • support individual faculty members to meet the requirements laid out in their research work in relation to Research ethics
  • strategy to make IT resources more scalable and reliable
structure
  • the organizational structure of DRD is to some degree highly decentralized
  • Administrative staff consists of a hierarchy and clear divisions (Finance, Admin, Tech)
  • faculty is managed separately
  • Within research labs, the faculty member in charge has almost total control over the organizational structure and practises and policies adopted including the information systems used, information management and security.
  • Individual professors are therefore key stakeholders to get on board for any organizational change;

Key Challenges

Challenges for the DRD arise from a variety of external, business, organizational, and information factors:
  1. Business (Teaching): The DRD must balance the need for revenue with the ability to deliver high value to its students.
  2. Business (Research):The DRD must attempt to remain responsive to the research needs of external agencies and funders.
  3. Organizational: The DRD has a decentralized power structure that makes organization-wide change difficult, especially given the scarcity of information and IT expertise.
  4. Information: The DRD must keep its IT infrastructure up to date with key requirements in security, storage and access subject to many stringent regulations

Key Opportunities

Business

    Service-Oriented Alignment

  • Service and support for other departments
  • provisioning excess research space into a physical service asset [Chui et al., 2013].
  • temporarily-provisioned DRD equipment
  • moving toward flexible service creation [Zammuto et al., 2007].

    Momentum for Change

  • institution-wide push toward information management improvement
  • Organization

    Acknowledgement of Need for Change

  • Operations Coordinator position created less than a year ago meant to:
  • make improvements to existing processes
  • create non-existing processes
  • ensure faculty's technology needs are met
  • focus more on data and less on IT concerns
    Information
  • New Infrastructure
  • room to grow within the department
  • new infrastructure to be held to higher standard
  • e.g. new laboratories
  • Harmonization
  • Convergence

Full-Neoliberalism and Service Delivery

  • purchase of services (website, storage, exchange servers, etc.) from higher levels
  • total architecture alignment with university wide IT complex
  • new requirements at org level

University 2.0

  • Sea change within the university: bringing the Academy to the Net Generation
  • Organizational strategic shift toward ‘real business’: informationalized and transformationalized research product services and data stewardship
  • Research relationships and partnerships opened and networked

Documentation and Knowledge Management

  • Knowledge management practises may help to codify and standardize procedures for infrastructure deployment and procedure.
  • Collaborative information systems can assist document storage and increase research efficiency.
  • Information systems documenting equipment and task management can address critical needs within the DRD's IT department.
